In 2026, Flipbook showed a UI generated entirely as live video by a diffusion model. It feels like watching a screen being painted for you, in the moment. It also costs a dedicated GPU per viewer, every second. I found that the magic and the cost are separable.

The idea in one sentence

The feeling of a "model-painted" screen is a behavior bundle — fluid layout, live motion, universal interactivity, bespoke content — and none of it requires generating pixels. An LLM streams a few kilobytes of scene patches; your own device paints them at 60fps. The model is the director; your device is the painter; the token stream is the brush.

Streams meaning, not pixelsOrdinary generative UI streams what components exist. Painted UI streams how a persistent scene evolves — and a local choreographer turns every change into motion.
Token pacing = choreographyElements mount the instant their JSON closes, mid-stream. The reveal isn't a loading state — it's the show.
Alive between interactionsAmbient motion never stops; scenes drift from dusk to night; imagery quietly repaints itself. Idle cost: zero.
Everything respondsEvery element carries semantic identity — exact hit-testing for free, and the model can make anything interactive with a one-line patch.

The economics

0server GPUs required
~12,000×less bandwidth than a 2 Mbps UI video stream (measured)
~1 kbit/sfull animated scene stream (measured)
$0cost between interactions — the scene lives on your silicon

Video-generated UI pays per user-second forever. Painted UI pays per interaction — a few hundred tokens — and on-device small models can take even that to zero.

What an independent review said

"A model can direct a continuously animated, interactive, persistent visual world using tiny semantic patches, while the user's device performs the actual painting. That is a real and compelling result." — independent technical audit, July 2026

Where it goes

Not "a cheaper Flipbook" — a runtime for cinematic agent interfaces. Agents declare semantic intent (compatible with declarative protocols like A2UI); an organization supplies its component catalog and motion grammar; a versioned choreographer enforces brand and accessibility; cached generated assets supply richness; and the question every agent product now faces gets an answer: once the agent has declared the interface, how should it come alive?
